# Evidence Guide — Module 10: Travel and Tourism (Viajes y Turismo)

> **Status:** Draft for instructional-designer review. Competency-based, audit-defensible. Every assessment item maps to UoC → element → performance criterion → Cinco Raíces root id. **No legal-sufficiency claims are made by this document.** Tagalog learner-facing wording is review-gated and marked `[FIL — pending G3 review]`.

## Unit of Competency

**Manage travel and tourism interactions in Spanish (CEFR B1–B2).**

The learner can plan trips, arrange accommodation and reservations, take part in activities and excursions, engage respectfully with local culture, handle travel emergencies, and ask and answer the everyday questions of an independent traveler — all in spoken and written Spanish at an upper-intermediate (B1→B2) level, using the module's key grammar accurately.

**Key grammar (drawn from the competency map for Module 10):**
- Future tenses — simple future (`viajaré`), future perfect (`habré terminado`), and the `ir a + infinitivo` near-future; future for prediction/probability.
- Prepositions of place — `en, sobre, debajo de, junto a, entre, frente a, dentro de, al final de, cerca de`.
- Transitive vs. intransitive verbs — direct-object verbs (`visitar`, `recorrer`, `fotografiar`) vs. verbs without a direct object (`nadar`).
- Imperative + courtesy register — affirmative/negative commands with `usted`/`ustedes`; softening with `por favor`, `le recomiendo`, `pakiusap` register.
- Modal verbs of need and obligation — `deber`, `tener que`, `hay que`, `necesitar`.
- Interrogatives — `qué, dónde, cuándo, cómo, cuánto/-a/-os/-as, por qué, cuál`.

CEFR band: **B1–B2.**

## Elements & Performance Criteria

> Module 10 has six elements (10.1–10.6). Element 10.1 (trip planning) is delivered across two vocabulary sessions (sessions 1–2); elements 10.2–10.6 map one session each.

### Element 10.1 — Plan a trip using future tenses

- **Performance criteria**
  1. States future travel plans in Spanish using the simple future (`viajaré`, `reservaré`, `costará`) correctly for person and number.
  2. Expresses a completed-by-a-deadline plan with the future perfect (e.g. `Para mañana ya habré terminado el itinerario`).
  3. Uses future-with-`cuando` / `en cuanto` + subjunctive to sequence plans (`Planearemos el itinerario en cuanto compremos los billetes`).
  4. Talks about cost, season and probability using the future of prediction (`costará más en temporada alta`).
  5. Produces a short oral or written itinerary naming destination, budget, dates and bookings.
- **Linked roots:** `m10-viajar`, `m10-planear`, `m10-itinerario`, `m10-presupuesto`, `m10-destino`, `m10-reservar`, `m10-billete`, `m10-vuelo`, `m10-equipaje`, `m10-temporada`

### Element 10.2 — Arrange accommodation and describe location using prepositions of place

- **Performance criteria**
  1. Makes or confirms a room reservation, stating room type and dates.
  2. Locates accommodation and facilities using prepositions of place accurately (`cerca de la playa`, `frente al malecón`, `entre la catedral y el mercado`).
  3. Gives the position of objects and rooms (`en el tercer piso`, `al final del pasillo`, `sobre la mesa`, `dentro del sobre`, `debajo de la escalera`, `junto a la recepción`).
  4. Interacts with reception/front desk to request and return a key and resolve simple lodging matters.
- **Linked roots:** `m10-alojamiento`, `m10-hotel`, `m10-habitacion`, `m10-recepcion`, `m10-llave`

### Element 10.3 — Take part in activities and excursions using transitive and intransitive verbs

- **Performance criteria**
  1. Describes excursions and day-trip activities in Spanish.
  2. Uses transitive verbs with a direct object correctly (`visitamos el museo`, `recorremos la ciudad`, `fotografía el paisaje`).
  3. Uses intransitive verbs correctly without a direct object (`los niños nadan en el mar`).
  4. Distinguishes transitive from intransitive use and chains simultaneous actions with `mientras` (`nadan… mientras… toman fotos`).
- **Linked roots:** `m10-excursion`, `m10-visitar`, `m10-nadar`, `m10-recorrer`, `m10-fotografiar`

### Element 10.4 — Engage with local culture using the imperative and courtesy register

- **Performance criteria**
  1. Gives polite recommendations and invitations using affirmative `usted`/`ustedes` commands (`pruebe usted…`, `disfruten…`, `conozca…`).
  2. Softens commands with courtesy markers (`por favor`, `se lo recomiendo`, `pakiusap`).
  3. Talks about local customs and food, advising respect for them (`respeten las costumbres locales`).
  4. Forms commands correctly for regular and stem-changing verbs (`probar` o>ue → `pruebe`).
- **Linked roots:** `m10-probar`, `m10-disfrutar`, `m10-costumbre`, `m10-saborear`, `m10-respetar`

### Element 10.5 — Handle travel emergencies using modal verbs of need and obligation

- **Performance criteria**
  1. Describes an emergency situation and states the required action in Spanish.
  2. Expresses obligation and necessity correctly with `deber` (`debes llamar`), `tener que` (`tienes que mantener la calma`), `hay que` (`hay que contratar un seguro`) and `necesitar` (`necesitas denunciarlo`).
  3. Knows and can name the right resource for the emergency (embassy, police station, pharmacy, doctor, travel insurance).
  4. Gives clear conditional advice (`Si pierdes el pasaporte, necesitas…`; `Si te sientes mal, debes…`).
- **Linked roots:** `m10-emergencia`, `m10-necesitar`, `m10-deber`, `m10-seguro`, `m10-farmacia`

### Element 10.6 — Ask and answer traveler questions using interrogatives

- **Performance criteria**
  1. Forms well-built questions using the full range of interrogatives (`¿dónde…?`, `¿cuándo…?`, `¿cómo…?`, `¿cuánto/cuántas…?`, `¿qué…?`, `¿por qué…?`) with correct inverted punctuation and accents.
  2. Asks for directions, schedules, prices and recommendations as an independent traveler.
  3. Responds appropriately to common traveler questions.
  4. Uses `usted` recommendation forms in service contexts (`¿Qué restaurante me recomienda usted…?`).
- **Linked roots:** `m10-preguntar`, `m10-cuanto`, `m10-cuando`, `m10-como`, `m10-recomendar`

## Oral / Written Question Bank

> Underpinning-knowledge questions with model answers. Each question is tagged with its element code. These probe *why/how* the grammar works, not memorized recall.

### Element 10.1 (future tenses)
- **[10.1] Q:** When do you use the future perfect (`habré terminado`) instead of the simple future?
  **A:** To say an action will already be **completed before** a point in the future, often with `para + time` (e.g. `Para mañana habré terminado el itinerario`).
- **[10.1] Q:** Why is `compremos` in the subjunctive in `en cuanto compremos los billetes`?
  **A:** Time conjunctions (`cuando`, `en cuanto`) take the present subjunctive when the action refers to the future that has not yet happened.
- **[10.1] Q:** How can the future tense express probability rather than a plan?
  **A:** The future can predict/guess: `El billete costará más en temporada alta` ("will probably cost more"). It states an expectation, not a fixed intention.
- **[10.1] Q:** Give the simple-future form of `viajar` and `reservar` for "I".
  **A:** `viajaré`, `reservaré` (regular: infinitive + `-é`).

### Element 10.2 (prepositions of place)
- **[10.2] Q:** What is the difference between `sobre la mesa` and `debajo de la escalera`?
  **A:** `sobre` = on top of / above; `debajo de` = underneath. They express opposite vertical positions.
- **[10.2] Q:** How do you say something is "between the cathedral and the market"?
  **A:** `entre la catedral y el mercado` — `entre` takes two reference points joined by `y`.
- **[10.2] Q:** Why does `frente al malecón` contract to `al`?
  **A:** `a + el` always contracts to `al`; `frente a` + masculine singular `el malecón` → `frente al malecón`.
- **[10.2] Q:** Which preposition phrase means "at the end of the hallway"?
  **A:** `al final del pasillo` (`de + el` → `del`).

### Element 10.3 (transitive / intransitive verbs)
- **[10.3] Q:** Is `visitar` transitive or intransitive? Show why.
  **A:** Transitive — it takes a direct object: `visitamos **el museo**`. The action passes onto a "what".
- **[10.3] Q:** Why can't you add a direct object to `nadar` the way you do to `recorrer`?
  **A:** `nadar` is intransitive — the action does not pass onto an object (`los niños nadan`). `recorrer` is transitive (`recorremos la ciudad`).
- **[10.3] Q:** How does `mientras` let you combine a transitive and an intransitive verb?
  **A:** It joins two simultaneous clauses: `Ella fotografía el paisaje **mientras** los niños nadan` — each verb keeps its own (in)transitive structure.
- **[10.3] Q:** Identify the direct object in `Recorremos toda la ciudad vieja`.
  **A:** `toda la ciudad vieja` — the thing being toured.

### Element 10.4 (imperative + courtesy)
- **[10.4] Q:** How is the formal `usted` command of a stem-changing verb like `probar` formed?
  **A:** From the `yo` present stem, swap to the opposite vowel ending: `probar` (o>ue) → `pruebe usted`.
- **[10.4] Q:** Name two ways to make a command sound polite rather than bossy.
  **A:** Add `por favor` and/or a softener like `se lo recomiendo`; use the `usted/ustedes` form rather than `tú`.
- **[10.4] Q:** What is the `ustedes` affirmative command of `disfrutar` and `respetar`?
  **A:** `disfruten` and `respeten`.
- **[10.4] Q:** Why is the imperative the right mood for travel recommendations?
  **A:** The imperative gives advice/invitations directly (`Pruebe…`, `Conozca…`), the natural register for a host or guide making suggestions.

### Element 10.5 (modal verbs of obligation)
- **[10.5] Q:** What is the difference between `deber`, `tener que` and `hay que`?
  **A:** `deber` + infinitive = moral/strong personal obligation; `tener que` + infinitive = practical necessity for a specific subject; `hay que` + infinitive = impersonal general obligation (no named subject).
- **[10.5] Q:** Rewrite "you must call the embassy" two correct ways.
  **A:** `Debes llamar a la embajada` / `Tienes que llamar a la embajada`.
- **[10.5] Q:** How does `necesitar` differ from `deber` here?
  **A:** `necesitar` + infinitive states a need (`necesitas denunciarlo`); `deber` states an obligation/should. Both can advise in an emergency.
- **[10.5] Q:** Give an impersonal obligation about travel insurance.
  **A:** `Hay que contratar un seguro de viaje antes de salir del país.`

### Element 10.6 (interrogatives)
- **[10.6] Q:** Why does `cuánto` change form in `¿cuántas paradas?`
  **A:** `cuánto` agrees in gender and number with the noun it modifies: `cuántas paradas` (feminine plural).
- **[10.6] Q:** Why do interrogative words carry a written accent (`cuándo`, `cómo`, `dónde`)?
  **A:** The accent marks the interrogative/exclamatory use, distinguishing it from the relative/conjunction (`cuando`, `como`, `donde`).
- **[10.6] Q:** What punctuation must open a Spanish question?
  **A:** The inverted question mark `¿` at the start, closing with `?` (e.g. `¿Cuándo sale el tren?`).
- **[10.6] Q:** Ask politely for a restaurant recommendation near the port.
  **A:** `¿Qué restaurante me recomienda usted cerca del puerto?`

## Critical Aspects of Competency

The learner must, for overall competence, demonstrate ALL of the following:
- Plans a trip in Spanish using the **future tenses** (simple future + at least one future-perfect or future-of-probability use) accurately.
- Arranges accommodation and locates places/objects using **prepositions of place** correctly.
- Correctly distinguishes and uses **transitive vs. intransitive verbs** when describing activities and excursions.
- Gives polite recommendations and instructions using the **imperative with a courtesy register** (`usted/ustedes` + softeners).
- Handles a travel emergency using **modal verbs of need and obligation** (`deber`, `tener que`, `hay que`, `necesitar`) and names the correct resource.
- Asks and answers traveler questions using **interrogatives** with correct accents and inverted punctuation.
- Sustains the above at **CEFR B1–B2**: connected, comprehensible spoken/written Spanish, self-correcting minor errors.
- Submits a portfolio of **Cinco Raíces "can-do" summaries** covering the module's 35 roots across the six elements.

## Methods of Assessment

Competence is assessed through a combination of:
- **Demonstration / direct observation** — the role-plays and tasks in the Observation Checklist (trip-planning monologue, hotel reception role-play, excursion description, courtesy recommendations, emergency role-play, traveler Q&A). Rated S / NY against observable criteria.
- **Oral questioning** — underpinning-knowledge questions from the Question Bank, delivered live to confirm the learner understands the key grammar (future tenses, prepositions of place, transitivity, imperative/courtesy, modals, interrogatives) and not just memorized phrases.
- **Portfolio of Cinco Raíces "can-do" summaries** — the learner's SRS (SM-2) study record and short self-authored can-do statements evidencing the 35 module-10 roots, organized by element.
- **Written task** — a short composed itinerary or travel email integrating future tenses, prepositions of place and at least one modal-of-obligation safety note; plus a set of written traveler questions with correct `¿…?` punctuation.

A learner is judged competent only when demonstration, oral questioning, and written/portfolio evidence are all Satisfactory.

## Context of Assessment

- **Where:** A classroom or a supervised online session (proctored video). Performance tasks may be done live or recorded for later rating.
- **Conditions:** Role-play prompts, a map or hotel-layout image (for prepositions of place), and a sample emergency scenario card may be provided. No script reading for the target performance; bilingual glossaries are reference-only, not answer keys.
- **Language of instruction:** English (working language of this guide) and Tagalog for clarification — `[FIL — pending G3 review]` for all learner-facing Tagalog scaffolding. **Spanish is required for the target performance**; the can-do tasks must be produced in Spanish at B1–B2.
- **Tools allowed:** The Torna Idioma SRS portfolio; a dictionary for the *written* task only; no machine translation during demonstration or oral questioning.
- No legal-sufficiency claims are made; placement and any cross-cutting unit (e.g. Rizal Studies) are config-driven and confirmed at program registration.
