# Evidence Guide — Module 6: Tu Cuerpo Es Tu Templo (Your Body Is Your Temple)

> TESDA-format evidence guide for instructional-designer review. Competency-based and audit-defensible: every assessment item maps to UoC → element → performance criterion. This guide makes **no legal-sufficiency claims**. Tagalog (Filipino) learner-facing wording is review-gated and marked `[FIL — pending G3 review]`.

## Unit of Competency

**Communicate about health, the body and well-being in Spanish.** (CEFR B1)

The learner can describe physical and mental states, interact in a medical/clinical setting, talk about healthy habits and nutrition, narrate exercise and physical activity in progress, report symptoms and receive/give advice, and express wishes and recommendations — using Spanish at CEFR B1 with the module's key grammar (ser/estar + adjective; direct- and indirect-object pronouns; comparatives and superlatives; the gerund / present continuous; the subjunctive for wishes and advice; the conditional).

**Module key grammar (from the TESDA Competency Map):**
- 6.1 — ser/estar + adjective
- 6.2 — direct-object (DO) and indirect-object (IO) pronouns
- 6.3 — comparatives and superlatives
- 6.4 — gerund / present continuous
- 6.5 — subjunctive for wishes and advice
- 6.6 — conditional

## Elements & Performance Criteria

### Element 6.1 — Health and wellness (ser/estar + adjective)

- **Performance criteria** (observable can-do statements):
  - PC 6.1.1 — Describes a permanent/inherent trait of self or others with **ser + adjective** (e.g. *Mi hermano es fuerte*).
  - PC 6.1.2 — Describes a current/temporary state of health with **estar + adjective** (e.g. *Hoy estoy enfermo*).
  - PC 6.1.3 — Distinguishes ser vs. estar correctly when both are possible with the same adjective, justifying the choice (trait vs. state).
  - PC 6.1.4 — Uses core health/body vocabulary (salud, cuerpo, bienestar) in complete B1 sentences.
  - PC 6.1.5 — Talks about physical AND mental well-being, not just illness.
- **Linked roots:** `m6-salud`, `m6-cuerpo`, `m6-bienestar`, `m6-enfermo`, `m6-fuerte`

### Element 6.2 — At the doctor's office (DO/IO pronouns)

- **Performance criteria**:
  - PC 6.2.1 — Replaces a named direct object with the correct **DO pronoun** (lo/la/los/las) — e.g. *La receta → el médico me **la** da*.
  - PC 6.2.2 — Uses the correct **IO pronoun** (me/te/le/nos/les) to mark the recipient — e.g. *El doctor **me** escucha el corazón*.
  - PC 6.2.3 — Combines IO + DO pronouns in order and applies the **le/les → se** rule before lo/la (e.g. *se las explico al médico*).
  - PC 6.2.4 — Names body parts and clinic vocabulary (cabeza, corazón, médico, cita, receta) accurately.
  - PC 6.2.5 — Conducts a short clinic exchange (make/confirm an appointment, report a problem, receive a prescription).
- **Linked roots:** `m6-cabeza`, `m6-corazon`, `m6-medico`, `m6-cita`, `m6-receta`, `m6-examinar`, `m6-recetar`, `m6-doler`, `m6-curar`, `m6-medicina`

### Element 6.3 — Healthy habits and nutrition (comparatives/superlatives)

- **Performance criteria**:
  - PC 6.3.1 — Forms comparatives of superiority/inferiority (**más/menos … que**) — e.g. *Las verduras son más sanas que la comida frita*.
  - PC 6.3.2 — Forms comparatives of equality (**tan … como**, **tanto/-a/-os/-as … como**) — e.g. *Beber agua es tan necesario como dormir bien*.
  - PC 6.3.3 — Forms the superlative (**el/la … más … de**) — e.g. *El mango es la fruta más dulce de Filipinas*.
  - PC 6.3.4 — Uses irregular comparatives where relevant (mejor/peor) when discussing diet.
  - PC 6.3.5 — Uses nutrition vocabulary (alimentación, verdura, fruta, agua, azúcar) to compare food choices for health.
- **Linked roots:** `m6-alimentacion`, `m6-verdura`, `m6-fruta`, `m6-agua`, `m6-azucar`

### Element 6.4 — Sports and exercise (gerund / present continuous)

- **Performance criteria**:
  - PC 6.4.1 — Forms the gerund of -ar/-er/-ir verbs correctly (hablando, corriendo, viviendo), including irregulars relevant to the theme.
  - PC 6.4.2 — Builds the present continuous with **estar + gerund** to report an action in progress — e.g. *Estoy haciendo ejercicio ahora mismo*.
  - PC 6.4.3 — Narrates a current/ongoing physical activity at B1 (training, running, strengthening muscles, resting).
  - PC 6.4.4 — Uses sport/exercise vocabulary (ejercicio, entrenar, correr, músculo, descansar) accurately.
  - PC 6.4.5 — Distinguishes habitual present (corro cada día) from action-in-progress (estoy corriendo) when prompted.
- **Linked roots:** `m6-ejercicio`, `m6-entrenar`, `m6-correr`, `m6-musculo`, `m6-descansar`

### Element 6.5 — Symptoms and advice (subjunctive for wishes/advice)

- **Performance criteria**:
  - PC 6.5.1 — Reports symptoms accurately (síntoma, fiebre, tos) in complete sentences.
  - PC 6.5.2 — Forms the present subjunctive after verbs of advice/recommendation/wish (recomendar que, aconsejar que, querer que, sugerir que) — e.g. *El médico recomienda que **describas** tus síntomas*.
  - PC 6.5.3 — Uses impersonal expressions of advice with the subjunctive (es importante que, es mejor que) — e.g. *Es importante que **tomes** agua tibia*.
  - PC 6.5.4 — Gives at least two pieces of health advice to a peer using the subjunctive correctly.
  - PC 6.5.5 — Uses care/avoidance vocabulary (cuidar, evitar) inside subjunctive advice.
- **Linked roots:** `m6-sintoma`, `m6-fiebre`, `m6-tos`, `m6-cuidar`, `m6-evitar`

### Element 6.6 — Wishes and recommendations (conditional)

- **Performance criteria**:
  - PC 6.6.1 — Forms the simple conditional (-ía endings) on regular and common irregular verbs.
  - PC 6.6.2 — Softens a recommendation/advice with the conditional for courtesy — e.g. *Yo te **recomendaría** un chequeo anual*.
  - PC 6.6.3 — Expresses a personal wish with the conditional — e.g. *Me **gustaría** sentirme sano todos los días*.
  - PC 6.6.4 — Uses **deber** in the conditional (deberías) to give a gentle "should" recommendation.
  - PC 6.6.5 — Uses recommendation/improvement vocabulary (recomendar, aconsejar, deber, mejorar, gustar) in conditional sentences.
- **Linked roots:** `m6-recomendar`, `m6-aconsejar`, `m6-deber`, `m6-mejorar`, `m6-gustar`

## Oral / Written Question Bank

Underpinning-knowledge questions with model answers. Each is tagged with its element code.

### Element 6.1

1. **[6.1]** When do you use *ser* and when do you use *estar* with an adjective?
   *Model answer:* Ser is for inherent or lasting traits and identity (*Mi hermano es fuerte*); estar is for conditions and states that can change, including health (*Hoy estoy enfermo*).
2. **[6.1]** Translate: "My grandmother is very healthy." Why estar or ser?
   *Model answer:* *Mi abuela está muy sana* — estar, because health is a current state. (*Es sana* is also acceptable to describe it as a lasting characteristic; learner should justify.)
3. **[6.1]** Give one sentence about mental well-being using *bienestar*.
   *Model answer:* *El bienestar mental es tan importante como el físico.*
4. **[6.1]** What is the difference in meaning between *es enfermo* and *está enfermo*?
   *Model answer:* *Está enfermo* = is sick right now (state). *Es enfermo* is non-standard for "is sick"; with people we use estar for being ill.

### Element 6.2

1. **[6.2]** What is a direct-object pronoun and what is an indirect-object pronoun?
   *Model answer:* The DO pronoun (lo/la/los/las) replaces the thing/person receiving the action directly; the IO pronoun (me/te/le/nos/os/les) marks to/for whom the action is done.
2. **[6.2]** Rewrite using a DO pronoun: *Compro la medicina.*
   *Model answer:* *La compro.*
3. **[6.2]** Combine IO + DO: "I explain the doubts to the doctor." → *Se las explico al médico.* Why "se"?
   *Model answer:* le/les becomes **se** before lo/la/los/las, so *le las* → *se las*.
4. **[6.2]** Where do object pronouns go with a conjugated verb vs. an infinitive?
   *Model answer:* Before a conjugated verb (*me la da*); they may attach to the end of an infinitive or gerund (*va a dármela / dándomela*).

### Element 6.3

1. **[6.3]** How do you say something is "more … than"? Give a health example.
   *Model answer:* *más + adjective + que* — *Las verduras son más sanas que la comida frita.*
2. **[6.3]** How do you express "as … as"?
   *Model answer:* *tan + adjective + como* (*tan necesario como*); with nouns, *tanto/-a/-os/-as … como*.
3. **[6.3]** Form the superlative for "the sweetest fruit in the Philippines."
   *Model answer:* *La fruta más dulce de Filipinas.*
4. **[6.3]** What are the irregular comparatives of *bueno* and *malo*?
   *Model answer:* *mejor* (better) and *peor* (worse).

### Element 6.4

1. **[6.4]** How do you form the gerund of -ar, -er and -ir verbs?
   *Model answer:* -ar → -ando (hablando); -er/-ir → -iendo (corriendo, viviendo).
2. **[6.4]** How is the present continuous built? Give an exercise example.
   *Model answer:* *estar* + gerund — *Estoy haciendo ejercicio ahora mismo.*
3. **[6.4]** What is the difference between *corro cada mañana* and *estoy corriendo*?
   *Model answer:* The first is a habit (simple present); the second is an action happening right now (present continuous).
4. **[6.4]** Give the gerund of *entrenar* and use it in a sentence.
   *Model answer:* *entrenando* — *Mi prima está entrenando para el maratón.*

### Element 6.5

1. **[6.5]** Which verbs of advice/wish trigger the subjunctive in the second clause?
   *Model answer:* recomendar que, aconsejar que, sugerir que, querer que, and impersonal es importante/mejor que — all take the subjunctive when the subject changes.
2. **[6.5]** Put in the subjunctive: "The doctor recommends that you (tú) describe your symptoms."
   *Model answer:* *El médico recomienda que describas tus síntomas.*
3. **[6.5]** Complete with the correct mood: *Es importante que (tomar, tú) ___ agua tibia.*
   *Model answer:* *tomes* — impersonal expression of advice + subjunctive.
4. **[6.5]** Give one piece of advice to someone with a fever, using *aconsejar que*.
   *Model answer:* *Te aconsejo que descanses si tienes fiebre alta.*

### Element 6.6

1. **[6.6]** How is the simple conditional formed for regular verbs?
   *Model answer:* infinitive + -ía/-ías/-ía/-íamos/-íais/-ían (*recomendaría, mejoraría*).
2. **[6.6]** Why use the conditional to give a recommendation?
   *Model answer:* It softens the advice and makes it more polite/courteous — *Yo te recomendaría un chequeo anual* sounds gentler than a direct command.
3. **[6.6]** Express a personal wish using *gustar* in the conditional.
   *Model answer:* *Me gustaría sentirme sano y lleno de energía todos los días.*
4. **[6.6]** Give the conditional of *deber* (tú) and use it for a "should" recommendation.
   *Model answer:* *deberías* — *Deberías dormir ocho horas para sentirte con energía.*

## Critical Aspects of Competency

To be judged overall **competent** in Module 6, the learner must demonstrate ALL of the following:

- Correctly distinguishes and applies **ser vs. estar + adjective** when describing health and states (6.1).
- Uses **DO and IO pronouns** accurately, including the IO+DO order and the le/les → se rule, in a clinic exchange (6.2).
- Produces accurate **comparatives (superiority, inferiority, equality) and superlatives** to compare food/habits for health (6.3).
- Builds and sustains the **present continuous (estar + gerund)** to narrate physical activity in progress (6.4).
- Forms the **present subjunctive after verbs/expressions of advice and wish** and gives real health advice with it (6.5).
- Forms the **conditional** to make courteous recommendations and express wishes (6.6).
- Uses the module's Cinco Raíces health/body vocabulary accurately and intelligibly at **CEFR B1** across all six elements.
- Communicates with pronunciation and fluency sufficient for a sympathetic interlocutor to understand without strain.

---

## Methods of Assessment

Competence is assessed through a combination of:

- **Demonstration / role-play** — the learner performs the can-do tasks in the Demonstration / Observation Checklist (especially the clinic role-play for 6.2 and the live exercise narration for 6.4), rated S / NY against the performance criteria.
- **Oral questioning** — the rater draws underpinning-knowledge questions from the Question Bank to confirm the learner understands *why* (ser/estar choice, when to use the subjunctive, conditional for courtesy).
- **Portfolio of Cinco Raíces "can-do" summaries** — the learner's per-session "can-do" statements and SRS (SM-2) vocabulary records evidencing the 30 module roots across the six elements.
- **Written task** — a short B1 writing piece (e.g. an advice note to a friend about staying healthy) requiring comparatives/superlatives, subjunctive advice, and at least one conditional recommendation.

---

## Context of Assessment

- **Setting:** Classroom or supervised online session.
- **Conditions:** Real-time observation of speaking tasks; written task completed under supervision. The clinic role-play uses simple props/prompt cards (appointment card, prescription slip).
- **Tools allowed:** Vocabulary prompt cards permitted for the warm-up only, not during graded performance; no machine translation during assessment.
- **Language of instruction / mediation:** English (working language of this guide) and Filipino. `[FIL — pending G3 review]` Tagalog mediation wording is review-gated before learner-facing use.
- **Language of target performance:** **Spanish is required** for all assessed can-do tasks; mediation/clarification may occur in en/fil but does not count as evidence of the Spanish competency.
